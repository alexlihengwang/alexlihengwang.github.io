Deriving the Sherman-Morrison formula

Jan 6, 2021

In this post, I am going to motivate an intuitive derivation of the Sherman-Morrison formula using Schur complements.

Recall the following fact (see also this Wikipedia entry on Schur complements).

Lemma 1. Let M∈R(n+m)×(n+m)M\in{\mathbb{R}}^{(n+m)\times (n+m)}M∈R(n+m)×(n+m) be a block matrix with blocks M=(ABCD).\begin{aligned} M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B\\ C & D \end{pmatrix} .\end{aligned}M=(AC​BD​).​ If AAA is invertible, then there exist invertible matrices P1P_1P1​, Q1∈Rn×nQ_1\in{\mathbb{R}}^{n\times n}Q1​∈Rn×n such that P1MQ1=(A−BD−1CD).\begin{aligned} P_1 M Q_1 = \begin{pmatrix} A - BD^{-1}C&\\& D \end{pmatrix}.\end{aligned}P1​MQ1​=(A−BD−1C​D​).​ Similarly, if DDD is invertible, then there exist invertible matrices P2P_2P2​, Q2∈Rn×nQ_2\in{\mathbb{R}}^{n\times n}Q2​∈Rn×n such that P2MQ2=(AD−CA−1B).\begin{aligned} P_2 M Q_2 = \begin{pmatrix} A &\\&D - CA^{-1}B \end{pmatrix}.\end{aligned}P2​MQ2​=(A​D−CA−1B​).​

For the purposes of this post, we won’t need to know what PiP_iPi​ and QiQ_iQi​ look like. It is enough to know that these matrices and their inverses have simple forms.

We will apply Lemma 1 to the matrix (Abc⊺1),\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} A & b\\ c^\intercal & 1 \end{pmatrix},\end{aligned}(Ac⊺​b1​),​ where A∈Rn×nA\in{\mathbb{R}}^{n\times n}A∈Rn×n is invertible and bbb, c∈Rnc\in{\mathbb{R}}^nc∈Rn. For notational convenience, let P=P1P2−1P = P_1P_2^{-1}P=P1​P2−1​ and Q=Q2−1Q1Q = Q_2^{-1}Q_1Q=Q2−1​Q1​. We have that (A−bc⊺1)=P(A1−c⊺A−1b)Q.\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} A - bc^\intercal & \\ & 1 \end{pmatrix} = P \begin{pmatrix} A & \\ & 1 - c^\intercal A^{-1}b \end{pmatrix}Q.\end{aligned}(A−bc⊺​1​)=P(A​1−c⊺A−1b​)Q.​ This identity tells us that A−bc⊺A-bc^\intercalA−bc⊺ is invertible if and only if 1−c⊺A−1b1 - c^\intercal A^{-1}b1−c⊺A−1b is invertible, i.e., nonzero. Finally, inverting both sides of the equation, we have that ((A−bc⊺)−11)=Q−1(A−111−c⊺A−1b)P−1.\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} (A-bc^\intercal)^{-1} & \\ & 1 \end{pmatrix} = Q^{-1}\begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} &\\ & \tfrac{1}{1- c^\intercal A^{-1}b} \end{pmatrix}P^{-1}.\end{aligned}((A−bc⊺)−1​1​)=Q−1(A−1​1−c⊺A−1b1​​)P−1.​ In particular, the top left block of the matrix on the right is the inverse of A−bc⊺A - bc^\intercalA−bc⊺.

Looking back at the derivation, the main idea was to use a change of variables (the PiP_iPi​, QiQ_iQi​ matrices) to “shift” the inversion of A−bc⊺A - bc^\intercalA−bc⊺, an a priori difficult task, to the inversion of the scalar 1−c⊺A−1b1 - c^\intercal A^{-1}b1−c⊺A−1b, a much simpler task. This generalizes in a number of ways. For example, the same derivation applied to the (n+m)×(n+m)(n+m)\times(n+m)(n+m)×(n+m) dimensional block matrix (ABC⊺Im),\begin{aligned} \begin{pmatrix} A & B\\ C^\intercal & I_m \end{pmatrix},\end{aligned}(AC⊺​BIm​​),​ where A∈Rn×nA\in{\mathbb{R}}^{n\times n}A∈Rn×n is invertible and BBB, C∈Rn×mC\in{\mathbb{R}}^{n\times m}C∈Rn×m, gives a formula for the inverse of A−BC⊺A - BC^\intercalA−BC⊺ in terms of the inverses of AAA and I−C⊺A−1BI - C^\intercal A^{-1}BI−C⊺A−1B. This latter formula is known as the Woodbury matrix identity.
