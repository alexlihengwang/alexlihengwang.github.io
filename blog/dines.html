Dines’ Theorem

Theorem 1 (Dines (1941)). Let A1A_1A1​, A2∈SnA_2\in{\mathbb{S}}^nA2​∈Sn be n×nn\times nn×n real symmetric matrices. Then the image of Rn{\mathbb{R}}^nRn under the quadratic map Q:Rn→R2Q:{\mathbb{R}}^n\to{\mathbb{R}}^2Q:Rn→R2, given by Q:x↦(x⊺A1xx⊺A2x),\begin{aligned} Q:x\mapsto \begin{pmatrix} x^\intercal A_1 x\\ x^\intercal A_2 x \end{pmatrix},\end{aligned}Q:x↦(x⊺A1​xx⊺A2​x​),​ is convex.

This is pretty surprising in my opinion: There are obvious counterexamples if one attempts to generalize this result. For example if one allows inhomogeneity, then the following set {(xx2): x∈R}\begin{aligned} \left\{\begin{pmatrix} x\\ x^2 \end{pmatrix}:\, x\in{\mathbb{R}}\right\}\end{aligned}{(xx2​):x∈R}​ is the graph of a parabola and is nonconvex. What breaks for three quadratic forms?

If we look over the above proof line by line to see what breaks when we go from two quadratic forms to three quadratic forms, we will see that not too much breaks. Obviously, something does break because of the counterexample we saw earlier, but in fact the entirety of the proof remains intact save the very last sentence.

Specifically, in the case that Q(x)Q(x)Q(x) and Q(y)∈R3Q(y)\in{\mathbb{R}}^3Q(y)∈R3 are not collinear, we can still find a continuous path between Q(x)Q(x)Q(x) and Q(y)Q(y)Q(y) contained in the image Q(Rn)Q({\mathbb{R}}^n)Q(Rn) and bounded away from zero in the direction vvv. Unfortunately, we may no longer conclude that the interval [Q(x),Q(y)][Q(x), Q(y)][Q(x),Q(y)] is contained in Q(Rn)Q({\mathbb{R}}^n)Q(Rn), however, as this continuous path may loop, so that it is not contained in the same plane as Q(x)Q(x)Q(x) and Q(y)Q(y)Q(y). The power of Cn{\mathbb{C}}^nCn

Curiously, the three quadratic forms generalization of Dines’ Theorem is true when we switch from Rn{\mathbb{R}}^nRn to Cn{\mathbb{C}}^nCn.
